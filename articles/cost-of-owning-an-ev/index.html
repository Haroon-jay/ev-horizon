The Real Cost of Owning an EV: What Nobody Tells You

Buying Guides EV Horizon Editorial• Updated May 15, 2026• 8 min read

EVs cost more to buy and less to run — but by how much, exactly? Here is the honest math on charging, maintenance, insurance and depreciation.

The sticker price is only half the story

Comparable EVs still typically cost 10–25% more to buy than their gasoline equivalents, though the gap narrows every year and disappears entirely in some segments. But purchase price is the worst possible way to compare cars, because the costs that follow — energy, maintenance, repairs — run heavily in the EV’s favor. Total cost of ownership (TCO) over five years is the only comparison that matters.

Incentives complicate the picture in a good way: purchase rebates, tax credits, reduced registration fees and free city parking exist in many regions and can erase the price gap on day one. Always check what applies locally before ruling an EV out.

Energy: where EVs quietly win every month

Drive 1,500 km a month in a typical gasoline crossover (8 L/100km at $1.60/L) and you spend about $192 on fuel. The same distance in an efficient EV charged at home (17 kWh/100km at $0.15/kWh) costs about $38. That is roughly $150 a month — $1,800 a year — before off-peak tariffs make it even cheaper.

The caveat: rely mostly on DC fast charging at $0.45/kWh and the EV advantage shrinks to modest. The rule is simple — home charging makes an EV dramatically cheaper to run; fast-charging-only ownership makes it merely competitive. Maintenance: the forgotten thousand dollars

An EV has no oil changes, no spark plugs, no timing belts, no exhaust, no transmission service. Regenerative braking means brake pads routinely last well past 100,000 km. Studies consistently put EV maintenance at 40–50% below combustion cars — typically $1,000–$2,000 saved over five years. What remains: tires (which wear slightly faster on heavy, instant-torque EVs), cabin filters, and coolant service on some models.

Insurance, depreciation and the battery question

Insurance tends to run 10–20% higher for EVs, mainly due to repair costs and sensor-laden body panels — factor it in. Depreciation has historically been the EV weak spot, but it is stabilizing as the used market matures and buyers learn that batteries last. Speaking of which: the battery scare is mostly mythology. Modern packs are warrantied for 8 years/160,000 km and real-world data shows most retain 85–90% capacity after a decade — we break this down in Common EV Myths.

Buying used flips depreciation from enemy to friend: someone else paid the steep first-owner curve. Our Used EV Buying Guide shows how to do it safely.

The five-year verdict

For a typical driver with home charging, an EV saves roughly $7,000–$12,000 over five years in energy and maintenance combined — usually more than enough to offset a higher purchase price and insurance. Without home charging, the math still works in many regions, just less dramatically.

The honest conclusion: an EV is not automatically cheaper for everyone. It is dramatically cheaper for most people, and the only way to know which group you are in is to do the ten-minute math. Our calculators were built for exactly that.

Frequently asked questions

Is an EV really cheaper than a gas car?
Over five years, usually yes — typically $7,000–$12,000 cheaper in energy and maintenance for drivers who charge at home, which more than offsets a higher purchase price in most cases.
How much does it cost to charge an EV per month?
A typical driver covering 1,500 km a month pays roughly $30–$45 charging at home, versus $150–$250 in fuel for an equivalent gasoline car.
How much does EV battery replacement cost?
Out-of-warranty full replacements run $5,000–$15,000 — but they are rare. Batteries are warrantied for ~8 years and most outlast the vehicle, retaining 85–90% capacity after a decade.
Do EVs cost more to insure?
Typically 10–20% more, due to repair costs. The gap is shrinking as repair networks mature and insurers gather better EV data.
