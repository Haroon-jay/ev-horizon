Best Electric SUVs of 2026: Tested, Ranked and Explained

Reviews EV Horizon Editorial• Updated May 25, 2026• 8 min read

The electric SUV segment is the most competitive in the car world. These are the models that actually deserve your money in 2026 — and why.

How we rank electric SUVs

Spec sheets lie by omission. A big battery means little if the car is inefficient; a fast peak charging number means little if the curve collapses after two minutes. We weight five things: real-world range, charging speed across the whole 10–80% window, interior space and practicality, software experience, and total value — including the ownership math we walk through in The Real Cost of Owning an EV.

Tesla Model Y — still the default, for good reason

The refreshed Model Y remains the benchmark not because it is the best at any one thing, but because it has no real weakness. Efficiency is class-leading, the Supercharger network removes road-trip anxiety entirely, and the minimalist cabin hides enormous practicality — a deep frunk, a cavernous trunk, and rear seats that fold genuinely flat. The firm ride and button-free interior remain acquired tastes.

Best for: anyone who wants the lowest-friction EV ownership experience available today.

Hyundai Ioniq 5 & Kia EV9 — the 800-volt champions

Hyundai’s Ioniq 5 still looks like it drove out of a concept sketch, and its 800-volt platform delivers 10–80% charging in around 18 minutes — faster than rivals costing twice as much. The related Kia EV9 brings the same architecture to the three-row family segment, where it has essentially no direct competition at its price. Both pair excellent efficiency with the most usable vehicle-to-load feature in the business: your car becomes a power source for camping, tools, or a blackout.

Best for: fast-charging obsessives (Ioniq 5) and big families who refuse a premium-brand price tag (EV9).

BYD Sealion 7 — the value disruptor

BYD’s midsize electric SUV undercuts the Model Y in most markets while matching it on equipment and beating it on warranty. The Blade LFP battery tolerates daily 100% charging, the cabin feels a class above its price, and build quality silences the skeptics. Charging speed and efficiency trail the leaders — the trade-off behind the price. For the full context on BYD’s rise, read Tesla vs BYD.

Best for: buyers who want maximum car per dollar and charge mostly at home.

The rest of the podium

The Skoda Enyaq remains Europe’s quiet achiever — unglamorous, spacious, sensibly priced. The Rivian R1S is the choice if your SUV must actually go off-road and tow seriously, accepting the efficiency penalty that comes with it. And the Volvo EX30, while compact, deserves a mention as the affordable gateway into premium-feeling electric driving.

Whichever you choose, run the numbers for your own driving first: our Range Calculator shows what each battery size really delivers in your climate, and our Savings Calculator compares it against your current fuel bill.

Frequently asked questions

What is the best electric SUV overall in 2026?
For most buyers, the Tesla Model Y still offers the best overall package of range, efficiency, charging network and resale value. The BYD Sealion 7 is the strongest value alternative where it is sold.
Which electric SUV charges the fastest?
Among mainstream models, the Hyundai Ioniq 5 and Kia EV9 on their 800-volt platform are the fast-charging leaders, going from 10% to 80% in roughly 18–24 minutes on a 350 kW charger.
Which electric SUV is best for big families?
The Kia EV9 is the standout three-row electric SUV for the money, with genuinely adult-usable third-row seats and 500+ km of range in long-range form.
