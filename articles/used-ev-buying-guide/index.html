Used EV Buying Guide: The Complete Checklist Before You Sign

Buying Guides EV Horizon Editorial• Updated May 10, 2026• 8 min read

A used EV can be the smartest car purchase you ever make — or an expensive mistake. The difference comes down to one thing: knowing how to check the battery.

Why used EVs are a hidden bargain

Early EV depreciation was brutal — which means today’s used market is full of three-year-old cars selling for half their original price with most of their useful life ahead of them. Because EVs have so few wearing parts, a well-kept used EV is mechanically closer to new than any combustion car of the same age. The entire risk concentrates in one component: the battery. Master that check, and you master used EV buying.

The battery check: non-negotiable

State of health (SoH) is the number you need — the battery’s current capacity as a percentage of its original. Above 90% is excellent for a 3–4 year old car; 85–90% is normal; below 80% demands a serious price discount and is the warranty threshold for many manufacturers. Get the figure three ways if you can: the car’s own menu (many models display it), a dealer or workshop diagnostic report, and your own real-world test.

The DIY test is simple: charge to 100%, note the predicted range, and compare it with the official figure for that exact variant. Then drive 50+ km and check consumption against the predicted drop. Wild mismatches mean walk away. Before any viewing, run our Battery Health Checker to estimate what condition a car of that age and mileage should be in.

Paperwork: the warranty is the safety net

Nearly every EV carries a separate battery warranty — typically 8 years/160,000 km, guaranteeing replacement or repair below 70% capacity. Crucially, it usually transfers to you as the second owner. Verify the start date, what the threshold is, and whether any battery service has been performed. Service history matters less for mechanical reasons than for software: cars regularly updated and serviced tend to have healthier packs and current battery-management firmware.

Check for open recalls, confirm any subscription features (some brands lock heated seats or driver assistance to accounts), and make sure both charging cables and, where applicable, charging-account transfers are included.

The test drive: what to actually look for

Beyond the normal used-car checks, do three EV-specific things. First, fast-charge the car during the test drive if at all possible — a healthy pack should pull close to its rated DC speed; one that cannot is telling you something. Second, test regenerative braking through its modes; juddering or inconsistency signals trouble. Third, listen at highway speed: EVs are so quiet that bearing and suspension issues hide nowhere.

Model-specific homework pays off. Early Nissan Leafs lack active battery cooling and degrade faster in hot climates; high-mileage Teslas are usually fine on battery but check suspension and screens; LFP-equipped cars (most BYDs, standard-range Teslas) degrade slower and tolerate full charging — context our charging guide explains.

The complete checklist

Battery SoH verified above 85% (or priced accordingly). Battery warranty transferable, with start date confirmed. Full charge range test against official spec. DC fast-charge test completed. Regen braking smooth in all modes. All charging cables present. Software up to date and account features transferred. No open recalls. Tires even (uneven wear hints at alignment or aggressive prior use). Price benchmarked against the new-price discount and remaining warranty.

A used EV bought on these terms is one of the best value propositions in motoring — see how the running costs compare in The Real Cost of Owning an EV.

Frequently asked questions

What battery health is acceptable when buying a used EV?
Look for 85% state of health or better on a 3–5 year old car. Below 80%, negotiate a significant discount — or check whether the battery warranty (usually 70% threshold) still applies.
Does the EV battery warranty transfer to a second owner?
In most cases yes — manufacturer battery warranties (typically 8 years/160,000 km) follow the car, not the owner. Always verify the exact terms and start date for the specific vehicle.
Which used EVs should beginners avoid?
Be cautious with early EVs lacking battery thermal management (notably first-generation Nissan Leafs) in hot climates, and any car where the seller cannot or will not demonstrate battery health.
