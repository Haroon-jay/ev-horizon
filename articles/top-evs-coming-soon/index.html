Top EVs Coming Soon: The Most Anticipated Electric Cars of 2026–2027

EV News EV Horizon Editorial• Updated June 2, 2026• 7 min read

The next two years will bring the most interesting wave of EVs yet — smaller, cheaper, faster-charging. These are the launches worth waiting for.

The affordability wave finally arrives

For years the EV market had a gap where its heart should be: genuinely affordable cars. That changes now. Volkswagen’s ID.2-family hatchbacks target a starting price near €25,000 with around 450 km of range. Kia’s EV4 and EV2 push the same philosophy, and Tesla’s long-promised more affordable model — built on a stripped-down version of its current platform — is aimed squarely at this segment.

BYD, of course, got there first: the Dolphin and Seagull already redefined entry-level pricing in Asia and Latin America, and updated versions with faster charging are spreading through Europe. The economics behind this shift are simple — battery prices keep falling, a story we explain in The Future of Electric Mobility.

The adventure segment heats up

Rivian’s R2 is arguably the most anticipated EV in North America: a midsize electric SUV with R1-derived styling, around 480 km of range and a starting price near $45,000. Its smaller sibling, the R3, brings rally-hatch energy to a segment that has never seen it. Both target buyers who found the R1S desirable but unaffordable.

Lucid’s Gravity, meanwhile, proves that a three-row family SUV can post sedan-like efficiency numbers, and Hyundai’s growing Ioniq lineup continues to set the benchmark for 800-volt fast charging in mainstream cars — a topic our charging guide covers in depth.

What to expect from the technology

Three trends define this generation. First, 800-volt architectures are going mainstream, cutting 10–80% charging times toward the 15-minute mark. Second, LFP and next-generation sodium-ion packs are slashing costs in entry models. Third, nearly every new launch ships with advanced driver assistance as standard — BYD’s "God’s Eye" system made sure of that by making it free.

Slightly further out, semi-solid-state packs are appearing in limited Chinese production models, the opening act of the story we tell in Solid-State Batteries Explained.

Should you wait or buy now?

The honest answer: if a current EV meets your needs and budget, buy it — there will always be something better in two years, and today’s models are already excellent. But if you specifically need a sub-$30,000 EV or a midsize electric adventure SUV, the 2026–2027 window is genuinely worth waiting for.

Whatever you choose, check the real numbers first: our Range Calculator estimates what any battery size delivers in your climate, and our Best Electric SUVs guide ranks what you can buy today.

Frequently asked questions

What is the most anticipated EV of 2026?
The Rivian R2 and the next affordable Tesla model are the most searched upcoming EVs globally, with the VW ID.2 family leading interest in Europe.
Will EV prices come down in 2026 and 2027?
Yes — entry-level prices are falling as battery costs decline and competition intensifies. Several credible models are targeting the $25,000–$35,000 range, a price band that barely existed in 2023.
Is it worth waiting for solid-state batteries?
Probably not. True mass-market solid-state EVs are unlikely before 2027–2030. Current lithium-ion EVs already deliver 400–600 km of range, which covers the vast majority of real-world needs.
