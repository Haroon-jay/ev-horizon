The Future of Electric Mobility: What Transportation Looks Like in 2035

Future Mobility EV Horizon Editorial• Updated April 15, 2026• 8 min read

Forget flying cars. The real future of transportation is quieter, cheaper, networked — and much closer than you think. A grounded look at the decade ahead.

The boring revolution: batteries get cheap

Every future-of-transport story begins with one chart: battery cost per kilowatt-hour, down roughly 90% since 2010 and still falling. Below about $80/kWh — a threshold the cheapest cells have already crossed — an EV becomes cheaper to build than a comparable combustion car, no subsidies required. That single economic fact does more to determine 2035 than any concept car: electric simply becomes the default way vehicles are made.

Chemistry diversifies along the way: LFP for affordable cars, sodium-ion for the cheapest urban runabouts, high-nickel and eventually solid-state cells for premium range. Different tools, one direction.

Your car becomes a power plant

An average EV battery stores enough energy to run a home for two to four days. Multiply by tens of millions of vehicles parked 95% of the time and you get the grid’s largest storage asset — by far. Vehicle-to-grid (V2G) lets cars absorb cheap solar at midday and sell power back during evening peaks; pilots across Europe, Japan and California already pay participating owners hundreds of dollars a year.

By 2035, expect bidirectional charging as a standard feature and "your car earned $40 this week" as a normal app notification. The car stops being a cost that depreciates in a parking lot and becomes infrastructure.

Autonomy spreads — fleet first

As we explain in Robotaxis Explained, driverless fleets are already commercial reality in a handful of cities. Through the late 2020s they expand corridor by corridor: dense urban cores, airports, fixed shuttle routes, and — significantly — freight, where autonomous electric trucks on highway segments rewrite logistics costs. Personal cars gain ever-better supervised autonomy, but the unsupervised everywhere-anytime car remains the decade’s hardest problem.

The second-order effects matter more than the technology: if a ride costs less than ownership for city households, parking demand falls, and cities get to reclaim astonishing amounts of space.

Smart cities, quiet streets

The smart city of 2035 is less about sensors in lampposts and more about orchestration: traffic systems that talk to vehicles, charging hubs placed where the grid is strong, low-emission zones that quietly became no-emission zones. Add the underrated revolution — electric two- and three-wheelers, already the world’s most numerous EVs — plus e-buses and micro-mobility, and urban transport emissions fall even where car ownership stays high.

And the sound of cities changes. The loudest thing about a 2035 street may be its bicycles.

What to watch between now and then

Five signals worth tracking: battery prices crossing parity in every segment; bidirectional charging shipping as standard; robotaxi services posting per-city profits; charging plazas outnumbering gas stations in leading markets; and the first mainstream solid-state models arriving. None of these are speculative — all are in motion. The future of mobility is not a question of if, only of pace.

Frequently asked questions

Will all new cars be electric by 2035?
In several major markets, effectively yes — the EU, UK and others have 2035 zero-emission mandates, and China’s market is moving even faster. Globally, forecasts put EVs at 50–70% of new car sales by 2035.
What is vehicle-to-grid (V2G)?
Technology that lets an EV send stored energy back to your home or the power grid. It turns parked cars into grid storage and can earn owners money during peak-price hours.
Will people still own cars in 2035?
Mostly yes. Robotaxis will reduce ownership in dense cities, but personal vehicles — overwhelmingly electric — are expected to remain dominant in suburbs and rural areas.
